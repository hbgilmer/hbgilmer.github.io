Welcome to my website! My name is Humberto Gilmer; I am a sixth year graduate student in physics at the Ohio State University. A native of Houston, TX, I received my undergraduate education at Rice University in Houston (graduated May 2014), and recently completed the APS Bridge Program here at Ohio State (2014-2016). I study theoretical particle physics as a member of Linda Carpenter’s group; my research interests broadly include BSSM (Beyond-the-Standard-Model) physics and other topics in high energy theory and fundamental physics. In general, I'm interested in what is "truly" fundamental in our universe; how much of what we call "fundamental" truly is so and how much is just an incident of our 3+1 spacetime manifold? More specifically, I'm interested in proposing new searches for particles at the LHC, including supersymmetric and Dark Matter particle searches.

Research

Studying and researching particle physics means having fingers in several different pies. Here I provide an overview of my different research activities, ranging from my day-to-day work to the stuff that keeps me up at night.

Background: The Standard Model

The Standard Model (physicists have such creative names) is a "periodic table" of fundamental physics, including both matter and the mediators of 3 of 4 fundamental forces. Originally formulated in 1967, its "final" piece, the Higgs boson, was discovered at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) in 2012. In many ways it is a crown jewel of 20th century physics; it's one of the most accurate physical theories to date, and has withstood many attempts to break or replace it. The story doesn't end there, however. There are many ways in which the Standard Model falls short. There are phenomena it doesn't even pretend to explain, such as gravity, or dark matter. My research, and that of many other particle physicists, consists of efforts to extend the Standard Model to cover these areas, and in some cases to point in new directions to someday replace it.

Phenomenology

The LHC at CERN is one of the largest, most complex machines ever built by humans, but the concept is really simple. Many of the phenomena described by the Standard Model can only take place at extremely high energies. So to create those conditions, we smash protons going very, very fast (0.999999991c) together and see what comes out. The LHC produces a mind-bending amount of data (~25 pb/year); strewn throughout this data are the hints about what lies beyond the SM. The work of a phenomenologist consists of scrutinizing the data, looking for possible discrepancies and proposing new models to explain them. Then we test these models by running simulations (using a combination of software such as MadGraph, Pythia, and root) and see if our model can match what's seen at the LHC. I have predominantly studied aspects of parameter space of the MSSM; in particular my work has proposed new particle spectra, positing either the sneutrino or higgsino as the LSP and studying propsects for its discovery at the LHC, and implications on the rest of the spectrum.

Dark matter

The image at right was our first hint that all was not right with the world of gravity. The plot shows a galactic rotation curve; the further you are from the center of the galaxy, the slower you would expect things to orbit about that center, at least according to Newtonian gravity. But that's not what we observe! Instead we see the constant rate shown by the dashed line; this seems to imply that there's something we can't see that's exerting an additional gravitational pull and making things spin faster than we expect. Since it's matter, and we can't see it, we've called it dark matter. Thanks to cosmologists we've predicted that approximately 23% of the universe's matter content is dark matter (for reference, our ordinary matter is <\5%). My research on the topic involves modelling interactions between SM particles and dark matter, including new ways of detecting these interactions at colliders.

The hierarchy problem

Imagine flipping a pin in the air and having it land on its head and stay there, upright, with no outside intervention. In principle, there's nothing preventing this from happening, but if it did, we'd certainly have a lot of questions! A delicate dance of a similar magnitude takes place within the machinery of the Standard Model. The Higgs boson's mass is "corrected" by all the particles it couples to directly (basically all of them, except the gluon), so we'd expect the mass to be very large (~1018 GeV/c2). But that's not what we see; instead its mass is 125 GeV/c2, meaning all those corrections somehow have "magically" cancelled out. Is this an accident, or is there a deeper reason? Physicists have labelled this the hierarchy problem. Many solutions to this problem have been proposed, including supersymmetry, Randall-Sundrum models (which use extra dimensions!) or technicolor. Despite the many efforts to repair this problem, it remains a standing issue and an active area of research.

Wild speculation

While I'm running code or taking a walk, my mind will tend to wander into "dangerous territory". Some of these thoughts have included

  • What does E&M look like on different geometries? Start simple, like on a 2D sphere, and already things get weird (there are no point charges!)
  • The costal length problem, which amounts to noticing that the length of a coastline depends on how "zoomed in" your ruler is relative to the coast. This to me has "renormalization group" written all over it, in particular the notion that naturally-occurring structures have fractional scaling (or fractal) dimensions, which sounds an awful lot like the anomalous dimensions of QFT.
  • QFT has had unprecended success in its predictive power, but as any student of QFT will tell you it takes a lot of spit and prayers to get there. Is field theory the language of a fundamental theory?

METAPOST

When I first began writing papers and notes, there was no satisfactory out-of-the-box software to draw nice diagrams. There are some simple solutions, like JaxoDraw or TiKZFeynman, but they were unsatisfactory to my liking. After searching and searching, I settled on learning METAPOST. This is a program created by John D. Hobby at Bell Labs, and is an adaptation of the METAFONT language created by Don Knuth which is used to draw the glyphs in TeX and LaTeX. It has a rather steep initial learning curve, but once you get a handle on it, it can be very nice to use. All of my Feynman diagrams and a few others are drawn using METAPOST. Here I've included some of the resources I used to learn METAPOST, as well as a few examples of the figures I've drawn using it.

Getting started

As stated above, METAPOST has a rather steep learning curve. Making a figure is a different exercise than simply pointing and clicking. A nifty document called Learning METAPOST by Doing is a great introduction to using METAPOST by providing several compact, easy-to-do problems. In between each problem is a brief description of a different aspect of the language. The solutions manual can be found here.

Useful bits

Getting METAPOST to do what you want it to can be a bit of a pain at times. Here are a few tricks I've found to make working with METAPOST less painful

Diagram labels

METAPOST has no native support for compiling text labels in LaTeX. For the sake of consistency, this can be very annoying. Fortunately, extensive digging has revealed that sticking a small bit of code at the beginning of your METAPOST file makes it possible to compile LaTeX labels seamlessly: 


prologues := 3;
outputtemplate := "%j%c.eps";

verbatimtex
%&latex
\documentclass{amsart}
\begin{document}
etex
FeynMF macros

The FeynMF program (click here for the manual) is a self-contained package that employs a C++ program to generate the layout of a diagram and then METAFONT to draw the diagram itself. I briefly considered it alongside JaxoDraw and TiKZFeynman, but ultimately decided to learn raw METAPOST, as it's a more flexible and useful skill. Still, it's nice to be able to call on functions that other packages have already defined. In particular, it's nice to call on FeynMF's extensive library of propagator lines. To do this, first import the package using input feynmp. Then call on the function by invoking draw_(name of propagator); make sure you remove the parenthesis. A list of callable functions can be found on page 15 of the manual, linked above.
